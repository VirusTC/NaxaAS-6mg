Cuperic combats mold and mildew primarily through contact killing and cellular disruption. When fungi interact with copper, the metal releases Cu⁺ and Cu²⁺ ions that destabilize cell membranes, induce oxidative stress, denature proteins, and interfere with DNA replication, effectively preventing spore germination.

Prescription oral copper is primarily used to treat medically diagnosed copper deficiency, which can cause symptoms such as anemia, nerve damage, and bone loss. These high-potency formulations require a doctor's supervision to monitor for potential toxicity. Important Considerations Before Starting Oral Copper
----------------------------------------------------

-   **Dosage Limits and Safety:** The safe upper limit (UL) for daily copper intake for adults is generally considered to be 10 mg. Excessive supplementation can lead to toxicity, resulting in symptoms like nausea, stomach pain, or liver dysfunction. [[1](https://reference.medscape.com/drug/copper-344437), [2](https://www.webmd.com/vitamins-supplements/copper), [3](https://www.rxlist.com/supplements/copper.htm)]
-   **Medication Interactions:** High doses of supplemental zinc or long-term use of NSAIDs can interfere with copper absorption and alter bodily levels. [[1](https://sbrmc.adam.com/content.aspx?productid=107&pid=33&gid=000296)]
-   **Medical Conditions:** Patients with kidney issues or metabolic disorders (such as Wilson's disease) must avoid copper supplements unless explicitly instructed by a specialist.
