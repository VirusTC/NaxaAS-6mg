3\. Molecular Entry Pathways of Zinc-Induced Metallothionein Trapping

When high levels of exogenous zinc are introduced to the gastrointestinal tract, they trigger an intracellular feedback loop that inadvertently blocks systemic copper absorption, creating a localized trap.

```
       EXOGENOUS ZINC INTRODUCED TO GASTROINTESTINAL LUMEN
                               │
                               ▼
        Enters Enterocyte via ZIP4 Transporter Protein
                               │
                               ▼
     Binds to Metal Regulatory Element-Binding Transcription Factor 1
                               │
                               ▼
     Translocates to Nucleus ──► Binds to MRE Promoters
                               │
                               ▼
                Overproduction of METALLOTHIONEIN
                               │
                               ▼
    COPPER ($Cu^{2+}$) ENTERS CELL ──► Bind Irreversibly to Metallothionein
                               │
                               ▼
  Trapped Copper Shed in Feces via Sloughing Enterocytes (No Systemic Entry)

```

1\. Intestinal Mucosal Transport

Exogenous zinc ions in the intestinal lumen pass through the brush border membrane of the enterocyte primarily via **Zrt- and Irt-like Protein 4 (ZIP4)** transporters.

2\. Transcriptional Induction

Once inside the enterocyte cytoplasm, free zinc ions bind directly to **Metal Regulatory Element-Binding Transcription Factor 1 (MTF-1)**. This binding causes MTF-1 to translocate to the cell nucleus, where it attaches to specific Metal Responsive Elements (MRE) located within the promoter region of genes responsible for producing **Metallothionein (MT-1 and MT-2)**. [[1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0145305X23012107), [2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4139248/)]

3\. Competetive Metallothionein Expression

The enterocyte begins overproducing metallothionein, a low-molecular-weight, cysteine-rich protein designed to store divalent heavy metals.

4\. Selective Trapping Mechanism

Although zinc induces the production of metallothionein, the protein possesses a significantly higher thermodynamic binding affinity for copper ions (\(Cu^{2+}\) and \(Cu^{+}\)) than it does for zinc. When therapeutic copper enters the enterocyte through **Copper Transporter 1 (CTR1)**, it is immediately sequestered by the newly generated metallothionein molecules.

5\. Intestinal Shedding

The copper remains bound inside the cell cytoplasm, preventing it from reaching **Adenosine Triphosphatase 7A (ATP7A)** at the basolateral membrane for systemic circulation. Because enterocytes have a brief lifecycle of approximately 3 to 5 days, these copper-loaded cells are continually sloughed off the intestinal villi and excreted directly in feces, causing a severe drop in systemic serum copper levels.
